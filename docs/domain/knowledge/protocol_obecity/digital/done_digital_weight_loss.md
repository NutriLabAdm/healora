## Description

Digital lifestyle interventions (DLSIs) include mobile apps, web-based platforms, wearables, AI coaching, and telemedicine for weight management. Range from fully automated (stand-alone) to hybrid (human + digital). Evidence supports modest but clinically significant weight loss, with high scalability and low cost per user.

## Protocol Details

### Modalities
- **Mobile apps**: Self-monitoring, goal setting, BCTs, feedback loops
- **Wearables**: Activity tracking, HR, sleep, step counts (Fitbit, Apple Watch, Whoop)
- **AI coaching**: Conversational agents, JITAI nudges, digital twins
- **Telemedicine**: Video/phone coaching with dietitian or nurse
- **Web-based**: Structured programs, educational content, self-paced

### Key platforms studied
- Oviva Direkt (DiGA, Germany): app + dietitian check-in, −7.0 kg at 6 mo
- ARTEMIS (UK): self-regulation theory app, −1.85 kg vs control
- Lark, SureMediks, Greenhabit, PROTEIN: AI coaching platforms

## Evidence

| Outcome | Effect | Certainty | Source |
|---------|--------|-----------|--------|
| Stand-alone DLSI weight loss | SMD 0.26 (~2.6-6.6 kg depending on baseline) | Moderate | S055 |
| Mobile app weight loss | −1.45 kg (95% CI: −2.01 to −0.89) | Moderate | S053 |
| mHealth lifestyle (all forms) | −1.79 kg (95% CI: −2.3 to −1.3) | Moderate | S052 |
| Wearable-based | −1.08 kg | Low | S052 |

## Pros

- Highly scalable (reach millions vs in-person)
- Low marginal cost per user
- Available 24/7, location-independent
- Continuous data collection for personalization
- AI coaching can deliver CBT-based strategies
- Self-paced, user-controlled
- Reduces access barriers (transport, time, cost)
- No geographic limitation
- Can be prescribed/reimbursed as DiGA (Germany)

## Cons

- Modest weight loss vs in-person interventions
- High attrition (digital-only: 30-50% at 6 months)
- No stand-alone DLSI produced ≥5% weight loss (S054)
- Algorithmic opacity in AI platforms
- Privacy concerns with health data
- Digital divide: lower adoption in low-SES, elderly
- Minimal long-term data (>12 months)
- Lack of regulation for many apps
- Cultural tailoring often absent

## Personalization Data Needed

- **Digital literacy & smartphone access** — baseline requirement
- **Age** — older adults may prefer phone/video coaching vs apps
- **SES & education** — digital divide consideration
- **Motivation & readiness** — self-directed vs needing human support
- **Weight loss goal** — align expectations: digital ≈ −2 to −7 kg
- **Comorbidities** — T2DM, HTN (some apps target specific conditions)
- **Wearable device ownership** — integration potential (Apple Watch, Fitbit, Whoop)
- **Privacy preferences** — willingness to share health data
- **Preferred interaction style** — chatbot vs human coach vs self-guided
- **Time availability** — daily engagement vs weekly check-in
- **Language/cultural preferences** — tailoring needed

## Who It's For

- First-step intervention for mild-moderate obesity (BMI 25-35)
- Maintenance after initial loss from other interventions
- Individuals with no access to in-person programs
- Tech-savvy populations
- Supplement to pharmacotherapy or diet
- Bridge while awaiting specialist care
- Not recommended as sole intervention for severe obesity (BMI ≥40)

## Cost & Availability

- Low-moderate: $0-200/year (apps: free-$50; coaching: $50-200/month)
- Reimbursed in Germany (DiGA): Oviva Direkt prescribed/covered
- Employer wellness programs often include digital platforms
- Telemedicine: covered by many insurers post-COVID
- Free apps: MyFitnessPal, Lose It, Noom (freemium), Fitbit
- Noom: $60-200/year depending on plan
- Lark: AI coaching included in some insurance plans
- WHOOP, Apple Watch: $200-400 hardware + subscription

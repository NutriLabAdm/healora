## Description

Obesity is a chronic, relapsing disease requiring long-term multimodal management. No single intervention is universally sufficient — lifestyle, pharmacotherapy, and surgery produce synergistic effects when combined appropriately. This protocol synthesizes evidence on (1) lifestyle + pharmacotherapy combinations, (2) lifestyle + exercise combinations, (3) sequential/stepped-care algorithms from major guidelines (EASO 2025, AACE 2025, Canadian 2025, ADA 2025, NICE 2025), (4) combination pharmacotherapy (dual/triple agonists, multi-drug regimens), and (5) surgery + pharmacotherapy sequencing.

## Protocol Details

### 1. Lifestyle + Pharmacotherapy Combination

**Diet + exercise + GLP-1 RA** (33 RCTs, n=12,028, S086):
- Weight loss: −7.13 kg (vs −3 to −4 kg for lifestyle alone or drug alone)
- WC: −5.74 cm; SBP: −3.99 mmHg; DBP: −1.11 mmHg
- FBG: −6.51 mg/dL; HbA1c: −0.31%
- TC: −5.85 mg/dL; TG: −13.44 mg/dL; LDL: −4.78 mg/dL
- Fat mass: −2.93 kg; Lean mass: −1.29 kg
- Semaglutide/tirzepatide weekly dosing: most pronounced effects
- GRADE: moderate certainty for most outcomes; high for HDL, LDL, body fat%

**Anti-obesity drugs + exercise** (4 studies, n=202, S092):
- Orlistat + exercise: −2.27 kg more, FM −2.89 kg, FFM +0.56 kg, VO2peak +2.64
- GLP-1 RA + exercise: −3.96 kg more, FM −1.76 kg, FFM +0.50 kg (preserved), VO2peak +2.47
- Exercise attenuates fat-free mass loss during pharmacotherapy-induced weight loss
- Certainty of evidence: low to very low (limited studies)

**Key principle**: Pharmacotherapy + lifestyle produces additive/synergistic effects. Lifestyle alone insufficient for clinically meaningful weight loss in most patients with obesity. Pharmacotherapy alone loses lean mass; adding exercise preserves it.

### 2. Diet + Exercise Combinations

**Low-calorie diet + exercise** (97 RCTs, S090):
- Adding exercise to diet significantly improves: glucose, insulin, HOMA-IR, TG, HDL, SBP, DBP vs diet alone
- Aerobic exercise (especially cycling) + diet: best for cardiometabolic benefits
- High-intensity, supervised programs: most effective
- Metabolically healthy obese: greatest benefit from AE + diet
- Resistance training: no additional metabolic benefit over diet alone (except muscle preservation)

**Combined training (ART) in T2DM** (128 RCTs, S094):
- ART (aerobic + resistance): best for BMI, IL-6, TNF-α reduction
- PMT (physical-mental training): best for lipids (TG, TC, HDL, LDL), HbA1c, HOMA-IR
- VO2max improvement strongly associated with BMI and HbA1c reduction

**Diet + exercise vs diet alone for MetS** (46 studies, n=12,128, S091):
- Diet + exercise: MetS z-score −0.68 units
- Exercise alone: −0.63 units
- Diet alone: −0.72 units
- Pharmacotherapy: −0.30 units (but limited data, all OMMs + lifestyle confounded)
- Direct comparison: combined diet+exercise > diet alone (−0.75 units)

### 3. Sequential/Stepped-Care Algorithms (2025 Guidelines)

**EASO 2025 Algorithm** (S097):
- Complication-centric: treatment selection based on obesity-related complications, not just BMI
- First-line for all: lifestyle intervention (diet + exercise + behavioral)
- Add pharmacotherapy if lifestyle insufficient or complications present:
  - Tirzepatide/semaglutide: first-choice for glycemic complications
  - Semaglutide: first-choice for CVD, HFpEF, knee OA
  - Tirzepatide: first-choice for OSA, MASH, prediabetes
  - Liraglutide/naltrexone-bupropion: second-line
  - Orlistat/phentermine-topiramate: third-line
- Step up to surgery if pharmacotherapy insufficient or severe obesity (BMI ≥40 or ≥35 with complications)

**AACE 2025 Algorithm** (S095):
- Person-centered, complication-centric
- 11-section algorithm: screening → diagnosis → individualize → treat → monitor → intensify
- Hierarchy of preferred medications based on complication profile
- Lower-cost step therapy option included
- Emphasis on health optimization, not just weight reduction

**Canadian 2025 Guideline** (S096):
- Pharmacotherapy + health behavior changes for all
- Long-term (potentially lifelong) treatment
- Specific recommendations by complication: HFpEF (semaglutide), T2DM (semaglutide/tirzepatide), prediabetes (tirzepatide/semaglutide), OSA (tirzepatide), MASH (tirzepatide), OA (semaglutide)
- Against: compounded medications, non-approved drugs
- Surgery: BMI ≥40 or ≥35 with ≥1 complication

**ADA 2025 Standards of Care** (S098):
- Individualize initial approach based on medical history, preferences, motivation
- Combine approaches for higher-risk individuals
- Pharmacotherapy for BMI ≥27 with T2DM + complication
- Assess response: monthly × 3 months, then quarterly
- Early responder (>5% at 3mo): continue long-term
- Poor responder (<5% at 3mo): intensify (add agent, switch class, consider surgery)
- Weight regain upon discontinuation: 50-67% within 1 year
- Pre-surgery: GLP-1 RA safe, no increase in complications

**NICE 2025 Guideline** (S099):
- Tiered pathway: behavioral → structured programs → specialist services → pharmacotherapy → surgery
- Culturally appropriate, individualized
- Refer to specialist if: complex needs, failed less intensive management, VLCD needed, surgery considered
- Pharmacotherapy: continue if >5% weight loss at 3 months

### 4. Combination & Emerging Pharmacotherapy

**FDA/EMA-approved OMMs ranked by efficacy** (Nature Medicine NMA, 56 RCTs, n=60,307, S035):
1. Tirzepatide: TBWL% −17.8 to −22.5% (vs semaglutide SMD 0.75)
2. Semaglutide: TBWL% −13.9 to −15.8%
3. Phentermine/Topiramate: TBWL% −7 to −9%
4. Liraglutide: TBWL% −5 to −7%
5. Naltrexone/Bupropion: TBWL% −4 to −6%
6. Orlistat: TBWL% −2.6 to −3.4%

**Emerging polyagonists** (27 RCTs, n=15,584, S093):
- Retatrutide (GLP-1/GIP/GCGR tri-agonist): −24.15 kg at 52 weeks — approaching bariatric surgery
- Tirzepatide (GLP-1/GIP dual): −17.8 to −22.5% TBWL
- Mazdutide (GLP-1/GCGR dual): −11.3 to −15.7% (intermediate)
- Orforglipron (oral non-peptide GLP-1 RA): emerging
- Tri-agonists: 24.15 kg vs dual: 11.07 kg vs mono: 7.03 kg at 52 weeks (S093)

**Combination therapy principles** (S096):
- Single mechanism plateaus; combination targets multiple pathways
- Current approved combos: phentermine/topiramate, naltrexone/bupropion
- Future: GLP-1 + SGLT2i, GLP-1 + amylin, triple agonists
- Sequential monotherapy: switch class if inadequate response (vs. adding)
- No evidence yet for triple drug combination (GLP-1 + phentermine/topiramate + naltrexone/bupropion) — theoretical but untested in RCTs

**Safety** (S092):
- Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea: most common with GLP-1-based therapies
- Topiramate: depression (RR 1.62), anxiety (RR 1.91), irritability (RR 3.31), sleep disorders (RR 1.55)
- Naltrexone/bupropion: anxiety (RR 2.44)
- Liraglutide: sleep disorders (RR 1.50), GI AEs
- No medication increased suicidal events

### 5. Surgery + Pharmacotherapy Sequencing

**Pre-surgery**: GLP-1 RA safe, reduces operative risk through weight loss, no increase in complications or time to surgery (S098, ADA)

**Post-surgery**: GLP-1 RA effective for weight regain after surgical plateau (S098)
- Significant additional weight loss when weight loss has plateaued post-surgery
- Should be considered for inadequate weight loss or significant regain

**Comparative effectiveness** (S087, S088):
- MBS: 28.3% TWL vs GLP-1 RA: 10.3% TWL at 2 years
- MACE: HR 0.65 for MBS vs GLP-1 RA in T2DM
- All-cause mortality: HR 0.68
- Cost: MBS break-even vs GLP-1 RA at ~15 months
- Surgery superior for durable weight loss, cardiovascular protection, cost-effectiveness long-term
- BUT: perioperative risk, lifelong nutritional monitoring, limited accessibility

## Pros

- Lifestyle + pharmacotherapy combination provides additive weight loss and superior metabolic benefits vs either alone
- Exercise added to pharmacotherapy preserves fat-free mass (critical for long-term metabolic rate)
- Multiple guidelines (EASO, AACE, Canadian, ADA, NICE) now converge on stepped-care, complication-centric approach
- EASO 2025 algorithm is first GRADE-based, evidence-driven treatment algorithm for OMM selection by complication
- Triple agonists (retatrutide) achieve weight loss approaching bariatric surgery (−24.15 kg) without surgical risk
- GLP-1 RA safe and effective both pre- and post-bariatric surgery
- Sequential approach avoids overtreatment: start low, intensify based on response
- Diet + aerobic exercise combination robustly improves cardiometabolic risk factors beyond weight loss alone
- Canadian 2025 guideline provides specific complication-based recommendations (HFpEF, OSA, MASH, OA, T2DM, prediabetes)
- Lifestyle interventions improve MetS z-score (−0.68 to −0.72) more consistently than pharmacotherapy alone (−0.30)

## Cons

- Lifestyle + pharmacotherapy increases treatment burden (time, cost, adherence challenge)
- Exercise + drug combination evidence limited to 4 small studies (GRADE very low) — need larger RCTs
- Sequential algorithms require frequent monitoring (monthly initially) — real-world feasibility uncertain
- Triple agonists still investigational; long-term safety data lacking (especially cardiovascular)
- Weight regain upon GLP-1 discontinuation: 50-67% within 1 year — necessitates lifelong treatment
- Combination pharmacotherapy increases GI AE burden; topiramate has significant neuropsychiatric AEs
- MBS vs GLP-1 RA comparative data from observational cohorts, not RCTs — residual confounding possible
- Cost of lifelong GLP-1 RA ($900-1400/month) vs one-time surgery ($15,000-30,000) — affordability barrier
- No high-quality evidence for triple drug combinations (GLP-1 + phentermine/topiramate + naltrexone/bupropion)
- Most guideline algorithms based on expert consensus where direct RCT evidence lacking for sequencing
- Surgery + pharmacotherapy sequencing: limited prospective data for post-surgery GLP-1 use
- MetS z-score reduction by pharmacotherapy potentially underestimated because most OMM trials include lifestyle co-intervention in both arms

## Personalization Data Needed

- **Obesity-related complications** — the single most important factor per EASO/AACE/Canadian algorithms: CVD, T2DM, HFpEF, OSA, MASH, OA, prediabetes, dyslipidemia, hypertension
- **BMI and adiposity staging** — guides treatment intensity threshold
- **Prior treatment history** — what was tried, response, AEs, duration, adherence
- **Weight loss goal** — 5% (metabolic), 10% (T2DM remission), 15-20% (OSA/MASH), 25%+ (surgery territory)
- **Early response assessment** — >5% at 3 months predicts long-term success
- **Age** — affects drug choice (tri-agonist safety in elderly unknown), surgical candidacy
- **Psychological status** — topiramate contraindicated in depression; naltrexone/bupropion caution with anxiety
- **Fertility/pregnancy** — GLP-1 contraindicated in pregnancy; topiramate teratogenic
- **Cost/insurance** — determines drug class access, surgery coverage, step therapy requirements
- **Adherence profile** — weekly dosing (semaglutide/tirzepatide) preferred for poor adherence
- **Muscle mass/Frailty** — exercise + pharmacotherapy needed to preserve lean mass; avoid aggressive weight loss in frail
- **Cultural/dietary preferences** — guide lifestyle modification component
- **Access to specialists** — obesity medicine, bariatric surgery, dietitian, exercise physiologist
- **Patient preference** — willingness for injection vs oral, daily vs weekly, surgery vs lifelong medication

## Who It's For

- All individuals with obesity requiring management beyond lifestyle alone (i.e., most patients with BMI ≥30 or ≥27 with complications)
- **Lifestyle + pharmacotherapy**: First-line comprehensive approach for all
- **Exercise + pharmacotherapy**: Those concerned about muscle loss during weight loss; metabolically unhealthy obesity
- **Combination ART**: T2DM + obesity requiring inflammatory/metabolic control
- **Sequential step-up**: Those who fail initial monotherapy (<5% at 3mo)
- **Triple agonists**: Future option for those seeking surgery-level weight loss without surgery
- **Pre-surgery GLP-1**: Those preparing for bariatric surgery
- **Post-surgery GLP-1**: Those with inadequate weight loss or significant regain after surgery
- **MBS**: BMI ≥40 or ≥35 with complications who failed/inadequate pharmacotherapy
- **NOT for**: Pregnant/breastfeeding (most medications); frail elderly with sarcopenia (aggressive combination without exercise); those with mild overweight (BMI 25-27) without complications (lifestyle alone sufficient); those with contraindications to specific drug classes; those unwilling/unable to adhere to monitoring schedule

## Cost & Availability

- Lifestyle + GLP-1 RA: $900-1600/month (drug) + $50-200/month (dietitian/gym) — most effective first-line combo
- Lifestyle alone: $50-200/month — sufficient for mild cases but rarely achieves ≥10% loss
- Exercise + pharmacotherapy: $30-80/month (gym) + drug cost — preserves lean mass
- Sequential step-up: monitoring visits $50-200/visit × monthly initially → quarterly
- Triple agonists (retatrutide): not yet approved; expected $1000-1500/month if approved
- GLP-1 RA pre-surgery: insurance may cover 3-6 months; $3000-9000 total
- GLP-1 RA post-surgery: off-label in many countries unless T2DM; $900-1400/month out of pocket
- MBS: $15,000-30,000 total; insurance covers with medical necessity
- Global: GLP-1 RAs available in most high-income countries; access limited in LMICs; metformin + lifestyle universally available and affordable
- Cost-effectiveness: MBS break-even vs GLP-1 RA at 15 months; pharmacotherapy + lifestyle is most cost-effective first-line; step-up approach prevents wasteful spending on ineffective treatments

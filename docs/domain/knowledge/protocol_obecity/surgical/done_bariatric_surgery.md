## Description

Metabolic/bariatric surgery is the most effective intervention for severe obesity, producing substantial and durable weight loss with improvement/resolution of obesity-related comorbidities. Primary procedures: Roux-en-Y gastric bypass (RYGB), sleeve gastrectomy (SG), biliopancreatic diversion with duodenal switch (BPD-DS), SADI-S, and one-anastomosis gastric bypass (OAGB).

## Protocol Details

### RYGB (Gold Standard)
- **Mechanism**: Restrictive + mild malabsorption. 20-30 mL gastric pouch, 100-150 cm Roux limb
- **%TWL at 5 yr**: 25-32%; at 10 yr: 21% (durable, S050)
- **T2DM remission**: 60-80% at 5 yr; superior to SG (OR 0.60 vs SG, S046)

### Sleeve Gastrectomy (Most Common)
- **Mechanism**: Restrictive. Vertical resection ~80% of stomach
- **%TWL at 5 yr**: 18-25%; at 10 yr: 15.4% (greater regain than RYGB, S050)
- **Pros**: Lower morbidity, shorter operative time, no malabsorption
- **Weight regain at 10 yr**: 27.9% vs 13.7% for RYGB (S050)

### BPD-DS / SADI-S (Highest efficacy)
- **Mechanism**: Restrictive + significant malabsorption
- **BMI reduction**: −18 to −19 kg/m² (S047)
- **TWL**: Up to 35-40% at 1-2 yr

## Pros

- Most effective and durable weight loss of any obesity intervention
- Resolution of T2DM (often before significant weight loss)
- Reduced CV events and mortality (10-15 yr follow-up)
- Improved quality of life, mobility, mental health
- Cost-effective over lifetime (reduces comorbidity costs)
- RYGB: durable 10-yr results, gold standard for T2DM
- SG: less morbid, faster recovery, no malabsorption
- SADI-S: highest BMI reduction in NMA (S047)

## Cons

- Invasive surgery: 0.1-0.5% mortality risk
- Long-term nutritional deficiencies (iron, B12, vitamin D, calcium)
- Dumping syndrome (RYGB)
- GERD (SG worsens de novo GERD)
- Weight regain (especially SG: 28% at 10 yr)
- Revisional surgery (5-15% of patients)
- Cost: $15,000-30,000 (US) out-of-pocket; insurance with criteria
- Strict eligibility criteria (BMI ≥40 or ≥35 with comorbidity)
- Requires lifelong supplement adherence
- Marginal ulcers (RYGB 2-5%)
- Alcohol use disorder risk (RYGB: altered alcohol metabolism)

## Personalization Data Needed

- **BMI, weight history, age** — surgical candidacy (BMI ≥40 or ≥35+comorbidity)
- **Obesity-related comorbidities** — T2DM, HTN, OSA, NASH, joint disease, dyslipidemia
- **Previous abdominal surgery** — adhesions, approach
- **GERD status** — SG contraindicated in severe GERD
- **T2DM duration and control** — shorter duration → better remission
- **Nutritional status** — iron, B12, folate, vitamin D, albumin, prealbumin
- **Psychological evaluation** — screen for ED, binge eating, psychiatric stability
- **Substance use** — alcohol, smoking (RYGB increases alcohol sensitivity)
- **Medications** — anticoagulants, NSAIDs (avoid post-RYGB)
- **Pregnancy plans** — delay 12-18 mo post-surgery
- **Motivation and ability for lifelong follow-up** — supplements, labs, diet
- **Insurance coverage** — prior authorization, criteria

## Who It's For

- Adults with BMI ≥40 or BMI ≥35 + serious comorbidity
- T2DM with obesity (especially RYGB if poorly controlled)
- Failed medical weight loss
- Severe obesity with mobility/quality of life impairment
- Contraindicated: severe uncontrolled psychiatric illness, active substance abuse, unstable CAD (optimize first), cirrhosis (advanced), pregnancy, eating disorder (untreated), inability to adhere to follow-up

## Cost & Availability

- US: $15,000-30,000 (insurance with criteria); self-pay options abroad $5,000-12,000
- Insurance: Medicare/Medicaid covers with BMI ≥35+comorbidity; commercial varies
- Global: covered in most public health systems (UK NHS, Canada, EU) with wait times 6-24 mo
- Lifetime supplement cost: $50-150/month
- Follow-up: lifelong multidisciplinary (surgeon, dietitian, psychologist)

## Description

CBT for weight loss (CBTWL) combines cognitive restructuring (identifying and changing maladaptive thoughts about food, eating, and body image) with behavioral techniques (self-monitoring, goal setting, stimulus control, problem-solving). Typically delivered in group format over 10-24 sessions.

## Protocol Details

- **Format**: Group (preferred) or individual, therapist-led or guided self-help
- **Duration**: 10-24 sessions over 10 weeks to 12 months
- **Core techniques**: Self-monitoring (food diary + weight), cognitive restructuring, stimulus control, goal setting, problem-solving, relapse prevention
- **Booster sessions**: Monthly follow-up recommended for weight maintenance
- **Adherence**: 70-85% session completion in RCTs

## Evidence

| Outcome | Effect | Certainty | Source |
|---------|--------|-----------|--------|
| Weight loss vs no treatment | Hedges' g = 0.31 (CBT best among psychotherapies) | High | S025, S027 |
| Weight loss vs usual care | −1.70 kg (95% CI: −2.52 to −0.86) | High | S026 |
| Cognitive restraint (improvement) | SMD = 0.72 | Moderate | S026 |
| Emotional eating (reduction) | SMD = −0.32 | Moderate | S026 |
| Effectiveness at 12-24 months | Sustained vs standard behavioral treatment | Moderate | S028 |
| Binge eating (BED) | Superior to behavioral weight loss for binge reduction | High | S029 |

## Pros

- Addresses root psychological drivers of overeating
- Skills generalize beyond eating (stress management, problem-solving)
- Long-term weight maintenance better than diet-only approaches
- Effective for binge eating disorder (BED) comorbid with obesity
- 3rd wave (ACT) shows sustained effect beyond 18 months (S028)
- Works across diverse populations and settings

## Cons

- Requires trained therapist (access barrier)
- Time-intensive (10-24 sessions)
- Weight loss magnitude modest (−1.7 to −5 kg) vs dietary interventions
- Less effective for T2DM glycemic control (non-significant HbA1c, S027)
- Insurance coverage varies by country
- Early dropout 15-30%

## Personalization Data Needed

- **Psychological assessment** — depression, anxiety, ED screening (EAT-26, BES)
- **Binge eating frequency & severity** — determine if BED-focused CBT needed
- **Previous weight loss attempts & outcomes** — history of yo-yo dieting
- **Motivation stage** — readiness for change (transtheoretical model)
- **Cognitive style** — rigid vs flexible thinking patterns
- **Social support** — partner/family involvement
- **Stress levels & coping mechanisms** — emotional eating triggers
- **Work/schedule flexibility** — group session attendance
- **Technology access** — online CBT options (telehealth)

## Who It's For

- Adults with overweight/obesity + psychological barriers (emotional eating, binge eating)
- Obesity with comorbid depression or anxiety
- Binge eating disorder with obesity (CBT is first-line)
- Individuals who failed dietary interventions alone
- Weight maintenance after initial diet-induced loss
- Contraindicated: severe untreated psychiatric illness (stabilize first), active substance use disorder

## Cost & Availability

- Moderate-high: $100-250/session (therapist); $500-2000 total program
- Insurance may cover if billed for BED or comorbid condition
- Group-based: lower cost ($30-60/session)
- Telehealth options available
- Self-help guided: lower cost (workbooks + brief coaching)
- Books: "The Beck Diet Solution" (Beck), "The Weight Loss CBT Workbook"

## References

- S025: J Clin Psychol Med Settings 2024 — NMA, CBT most effective among psychotherapies
- S026: PubMed 2018 — 12 RCTs, 6805 participants, CBTWL weight −1.70 kg
- S027: Sci Rep 2023 — 9 RCTs, 902 participants, CBT for lifestyle in obesity+T2DM
